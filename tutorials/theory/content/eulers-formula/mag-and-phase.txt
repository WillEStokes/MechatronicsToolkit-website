<p>
Complex numbers can also be represented using magnitude and phase, known as polar form representation. Using Euler's formula, a complex number can be represented as \(z = r(\cos{\theta} + i\sin{\theta})\), where \(r\) is the magnitude, and \(\theta\) is the phase. If we take the derivative of \(z\) with respect to time, \(t\), we get:
</p>
<p>
$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{dr}{dt}(\cos(\theta) + i \sin(\theta)) + r\frac{d\theta}{dt}(-\sin(\theta) + i \cos(\theta))$$
</p>
<p>
Using Euler's formula, we can write this as:
</p>
<p>
$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{dr}{dt}e^{i\theta} + ir\frac{d\theta}{dt}e^{i\theta}$$
</p>
<p>
We can see from this expression that the real part is equal to \(\frac{dr}{dt}\) and the imaginary part is equal to \(r\frac{d\theta}{dt}\). Thus, by differentiating \(z\), we can extract both the magnitude and the phase derivatives.
</p>
<p>
The magnitude, \(r\), is defined as the distance of the complex number from the origin on the complex plane, and can be calculated as:
</p>
<p>
$$r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$$
</p>
<p>
Where \(x\) and \(y\) are the real and imaginary components of the complex number.
</p>
<p>
The phase, \(\theta\), is defined as the angle between the positive real axis and the line connecting the origin to the complex number. It can be calculated as:
</p>
<p>
$$\theta = \arctan{\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)}$$
</p>
<p>
By using the magnitude and phase, it is possible to simplify complex operations and analysis.
</p>
